You get free college! And you get free college! And you!

February XX, 2020

Motivated by rising tuition and increasing student loan balances, local, state, and federal policymakers are considering new ways to reduce college costs, especially for low- and middle-income students.

Cities and states have already introduced several “free college” plans, primarily in the form of College Promise programs. Nearly half of all states have a program that provides tuition-free college for some segment of its population. And its seems like nearly every Democratic presidential candidate has a plan to provide more students with free or low-cost education.

A federal free college program could dramatically increase the number of students who could attend college for free, but much depends on the choices legislators make. Which students, and which institutions, will be eligible? Which college expenses will be covered? Our interactive shows how the answers to these questions dictate which students would benefit from a broad free college plan.

Although this simplified dataset doesn’t precisely indicate which students will get free college, we use this 100-dot pool to predict the broad effects of different policy choices.

Policymakers disagree on which college expenses should be free. For now, we will use “free college” to mean that students pay nothing in tuition and fees. Those costs are covered by merit-based or need-based grants.

As you can see, about 27 students (i.e., 27 percent of students) attending college in the United States currently have free college. These students receive merit-based grants (like Louisiana’s TOPS awards), or need-based grants (such as the federal Pell grant), and those grants were sufficient to cover the year’s tuition and fees at their school.

Free college is often proposed to “level the playing field” by making college more affordable for low-income students.

Looking at the share of students receiving free college by income, we see that some high-income students get free college, and many low-income students—especially students who are older or are independent of their parents—don’t.

Let’s consider two students currently receiving free college:

Elle

Elle is a dependent student attending a four-year public institution. Her parents’ annual income is $90,000, making her ineligible for federal need-based grants. She instead receives free college through merit-based grants because of her academic success in high school.

Abed

Abed is an independent student earning $35,000. He attends a two-year public institution while caring for his two children. Abed pays nothing in tuition and fees because need-based federal and institutional grants fully cover these costs.

Elle and Abed are attending different institutions and have substantially different economic need, but both currently have free college.

Now that we’ve seen who already gets free college, let’s think about the free college plan we might put in place. One of the key debates among Democratic presidential candidates is whether access to free college should be universal.

However, when states implement free college, they often impose an income ceiling for eligibility, like in Washington and New York. Similarly, we will implement a cap at or below 400 percent of the federal poverty level, which is $49,960 for a single person or $103,000 for a family of four in 2019. Anyone with a household income below that is eligible for free college under our plan.

This 400 percent cap is relatively generous so it’s no surprise that the number of students receiving free college increases dramatically to about 79 percent of students.

But many of these students attend private institutions, which typically aren’t included in free college plans. What happens when we limit our plan to public colleges and universities?

Restricting our plan to two- or four-year public institutions means that about 58 percent of current students would receive free college—leaving many low-income students with tuition bills.

Devon

Devon comes from a family of five. His parents earn $75,000 and he attends a four-year public university. Under this plan, Devon will now receive free college.

Justina

Justina attends a for-profit university. She is a dependent student earning $10,000. Despite her low income and a Pell grant, Danielle doesn’t qualify for the free college plan, and will pay for her tuition and fees with savings and student loans.

We can also see the effects of free college plans on students of different races and ethnicities.

Students who are Black or Hispanic are more likely to get free college under our plan. Although White students make up about 53 percent of college students, a smaller share of white students would receive free college under our plan.

Still, many currently enrolled students of color, like Justina, will lose out on the benefit of free college. One reason for this is the disproportionate share of black and Hispanic students at for-profit colleges. Students may opt for a for-profit institution because they live far from a public college or because the for-profit meets their needs better (e.g., by providing night classes).

Free college is sometimes touted as a means to help students reduce, or even eliminate, their need for student loans. If we provide eligible students with full tuition and fees, how might this affect the share of students who need to take on debt to attend college?

Even with free tuition and fees, student debt likely won’t go away. Of those who had free college before our new policy, a substantial share also had student loans. And many of those who receive free college under our plan may continue to rely on student loans.

Beyond tuition and fees, students also need to pay for housing, food, transportation, and, in some cases, childcare, while in school. Some students, particularly those in four-year schools, cover these costs with federal student loans.

Ana

Ana is a dependent student attending a two-year college. She lives with her family to help reduce her living costs while she is in school. Her family’s income of $30,000 qualifies her for free tuition under this plan. Ana will not need to take out loans.

Denise

Denise is an independent student earning $20,000 per year. She is living in an expensive city to attend a four-year university. Even though her tuition is covered under our plan, she will still need to take out loans to pay for her living expenses.

If we want to help students pay for living costs while they are in college, we may add a stipend to our free college plan. What happens if we change the definition of free college to include a stipend of $2,000 per year?

About X percent of students currently have their tuition, fees, and $2,000 worth of living expenses covered by grants and scholarships. If we add a $2,000 stipend to our free college plan, a larger share of students would benefit, meaning that some students could reduce their debt or work less to cover their living expenses.

Of course, offering all students eligible for free college an additional $2,000 per year substantially increases costs.

Instead, some policymakers want a free college plan that covers tuition and fees and allows students who already have aid to keep it and use it for living expenses.

The idea of broadening access to free college is an appealing and important one, but how it is designed hinges on our underlying goals as a country.

If we want to provide universal access to college, we may want to prioritize making all two-year public institutions free, since those institutions are open to all who enroll. If our goal is to reduce student loans, we need to consider funding living expenses for low-income students. And if we want to close gaps in college access for students of color, we may need to think about how to support students who are in non-public institutions.

These choices can change students’ lives, but may also have ripple effects that can shape higher education and federal and state budgets in significant ways.

About the data

For our analysis, we use a dataset made up of one hundred representative students, built using data from the 2015-2016 National Postsecondary Student Aid Study.
